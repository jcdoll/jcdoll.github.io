Graduate school (2006 - 2012) I was a PhD student in the Stanford Microsystems lab where I developed scientific instruments for the study of touch and hearing at the molecular scale. One of the issues with studying these senses was that our bodies are very good sensors - far better than most of the tools that we use to probe them. For example, the hair cells in our ear which allow us to hear can sense forces at the piconewton scale, about the amount of force required to break a hydrogen bond.

The devices I developed bridged this technical gap. The main focus of my research was designing and fabricating a better tool for studying the mechanics and kinetics of mammalian hair cells. Performance requirements include a spring constant of about 1 pN/nm, a resonant frequency of 20 to 200 kHz, pN-scale force resolution over that bandwidth and integrated on-chip actuation, all while operating in electrically grounded saline.

In order to achieve this level of performance I developed new methods for the design and thermal modeling of piezoresistive cantilevers. Next I integrated the sensors with on-chip piezoelectric (AlN) and thermal actuators and fabricated them in the Stanford Nanofabrication Facility. The resulting force probes were 300 nm thick, 1-2 microns wide and 30-200 microns long and were mass manufactured using standard silicon micromachining processes. The device and readout circuit were designed to allow simultaneous actuation and sensing, with -100 dB of actuator-sensor crosstalk rejection at 100 kHz. The force resolution of the piezoresistive sensors was within a factor of four of the noise floor set by Brownian motion. All performance metrics were 10-20x better than previous work to date.

Details of the thermal, mechanical and electrical design of the force probes can be found in my publications. My thesis includes the most behind-the-scenes detail if you are interested in extending my work. Along those lines, you can find slides from the majority of my presentations since 2005 here. Most of the content is out of date but there might be some useful nuggets buried in the slides.

While at Stanford I also wrote a book with my advisor, Piezoresistor Design and Applications. It was published by Springer in 2013 and covers everything from piezoresistor noise sources to fabrication process options and model to numerical methods.
